Historic Redlining and Segregation

In 1938, the Home Owners’ Loan Corporation created maps of Atlanta that graded neighborhoods from “A” (best) to “D” (worst). Mostly Black neighborhoods were marked with red ink, a practice called redlining. This meant banks refused to give loans or charged very high interest rates. As a result, Black families had fewer chances to buy homes, build wealth, or move to safer areas. These unfair practices shaped where people could live for decades.

Ongoing Inequality

Even though redlining is no longer legal, its effects are still visible. Many of the neighborhoods that were graded “D” are still majority Black and continue to struggle with lower home values, fewer businesses, and underfunded schools. On the other hand, neighborhoods that were given “A” ratings remain wealthier and mostly white. This shows how past policies continue to shape Atlanta today, creating a cycle of inequality that is hard to break.

“Atlanta remains one of America’s more segregated cities.” — Hovertown Visuals on redlining & modern demographics

Key Numbers

  • Over 70% of neighborhoods rated “D” (worst) in 1938 were majority Black. (source: Mapping Inequality)
  • Many redlined areas still have lower home values and income levels today.
  • Schools in under‐resourced neighborhoods often get less funding per student than schools in more privileged areas.
